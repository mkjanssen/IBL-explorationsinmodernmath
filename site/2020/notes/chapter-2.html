Chapter 2 Frequently Asked Questions

Question 2.0.1. What is this course about?

Mathematics is about the numerical, spatial, and logical aspects of God's creation. However, most people have a view of the content and form of mathematics that is limited to their K-12 courses. In content, many view mathematics as arithmetic, algebra, geometry, and maybe trigonometry, all pointing toward calculus. In form, many view mathematics as a discipline in which the questions all have one right answer, possibly only one correct path to a solution, with new ideas presented as if handed down from Mount Sinai. We'll see this semester that both of these ideas are flawed. Mathematics is really about a way of looking at a question, and reasoning toward a solution. It's a way of doing this, as Francis Su suggests, that helps meet basic human desires and can ultimately cultivate shalom in its practitioners.

Question 2.0.2. What are the course learning objectives?
  • Students will play with God's mathematical creation by solving a Rubik's cube and other mathematical puzzles. (CS)
  • Students will explore the notion of mathematical truth, and assess its place in understanding God's creation. (RO, CR)
  • Students will apply mathematical thinking to articulate a vision for a more just society. (CD, CR)
  • Students will identify, analyze, and present on beauty in mathematics. (RO, CS)
Question 2.0.3. What will we be doing on March 23 (other than celebrating Dr. Janssen's birthday)? In other words, what is the course schedule?

That's a really tough question. Given the exploratory/inquiry-oriented nature of the course, I can't really say what we'll be doing on a given day all that far ahead of time. However, I think it will look something like this:

  • Module 1 (Play): January 10-January 27
  • Module 2 (Truth): January 29-February 24
  • Module 3 (Justice I): February 26-April 3
  • Module 4 (Justice II): April 6-May 1

Also, the notes and activities on this site will hopefully be a useful tool for seeing what is coming up, as well as what you miss when you (unfortunately) have to be absent.

Question 2.0.4. But what am I supposed to get out of this course?

That depends! On a superficial level, this course fulfills several requirements. If you haven't taken a quantitative reasoning course, this course fulfills that core requirement. Or maybe you are seeking a middle school math endorsement; this course is required for that. Regardless of why you are in this course, I have several hopes for you.

  • I hope you have fun! I think mathematics is delightful, and not as dry or boring as is often presented, and I hope you get to experience that this semester.
  • I hope you experience both authentic, active struggle with new mathematical ideas and questions, as well as the immense satisfaction from working out a solution.
  • I hope your ideas of what counts as a mathematical question are expanded. We can think mathematically about almost anything, from games and puzzles to philosophy, truth, justice, and beauty. We'll explore these ideas while also acknowledging their limitations.
  • On a related note, I hope you ask lots of questions!
  • Finally, I hope you see the practice of mathematics as a form of fulfilling the cultural mandate. Even when we work to solve the Rubik's cube, we are inquiring into God's creation, exploring the hidden order and potential built into the fabric of the cosmos by our almighty God.
Question 2.0.5. What will we do on a typical day?

Each day will be an exploration, in groups of 2-3, of a sequence of mathematical tasks. We'll typically start by wrapping up any loose ends from the previous day, then move on to the new tasks for the day. We'll take the last ten minutes or so to present our responses to the day's tasks and discuss where to go next.

Question 2.0.6. How can I get help in this class?

Given the inquiry-oriented nature of the course, your main avenue for help is to ask questions of others in your groups. If your group is stuck, I'll be more than happy to start you in the right direction, but will do my best not to give any answers away. If you are unable to resolve your questions in class, I welcome student hours appointments in my office (SB 1608). I prefer that you book an appointment ahead of time, but this is not required. If my door is open, just walk in! If it's not, you may knock, but I may ask that you make an appointment for another time.

Question 2.0.7. What items of work will be assessed?

As described elsewhere, the course will be broken into four modules, plus the final project. For each module, there will be:

  • some journal assignments
  • group (in-class) work
  • a module final assessment (MFA)

The nature of the MFA will differ from module to module and authentically and summatively assess your mastery of that module's content. In lieu of a final exam, you'll do a final project exploring some mathematical topic that you deem beautiful and present during our final exam slot, which is 3:30pm on Wednesday, May 8.

Question 2.0.8. What are the journals about? When are they due?

Each journal will look a little differently depending on our current topic. Sometimes you may be asked to read a short article or book chapter and react to it. Other times you may be asked to solve a few puzzles and discuss the process by which you solved them. Still other journals may look even more different; you'll find out when you open them.

You'll have at least a week to think about each journal. Journals are officially due some Fridays at 5pm. Unofficially, they may be turned in until midnight the next day. Journals are due:

  • January 17
  • January 24
  • January 31
  • February 14
  • February 28
  • March 20
  • March 27
  • April 3
  • April 17
  • May 1

Journals will be graded pass/no pass based on your ability to follow the given prompts.

Question 2.0.9. How will each item of work be assessed?

Unlike "typical" college courses, there are no points or partial credit to be earned in this course. Instead, each item of work will come with its own specifications for completing the item at one of four potential levels, each of which is given an approximate interpretation below:

  • Exceeds expectations: this work is submitted on time, is extremely well done, clearly communicated, and could function as the official class solution.
  • Meets expectations: this work is generally correct, but either contains some minor mathematical errors which do not detract from the overall solution, or contains some unclear/incomplete communication.
  • Revision needed: the work contains some significant errors and/or the guidelines for preparing and presenting the solution were not followed, but you seem to be on the right track toward a passing grade.
  • Not assessable: the work was not turned in on time, is difficult to read, or contains significant enough flaws that you're probably better starting over.

Generally, E's and M's are considered passing, while R's and N's are not. Some assessments will be graded on a 2-level rubric (such as group presentations), some on a 3-level (such as the Rubik's cube quiz), and others on a 4-level (such as the other MFA's).

Question 2.0.10. If my work is graded on mastery, can I revise and resubmit to improve my grade?

Generally yes, though some caveats are in order.

  • Group work presentations will be graded on an M/N scale; if you have something productive to say, each member of your group will get an M, and if not, each will get an X. You may not revise presentations which are not assessable. There is no limit to the number of times your group can be selected to present, so you can accumulate the number of successful presentations in line with your goal by repeatedly volunteering to present.
  • The journals will be graded on an M/R/N scale. You may cash in a token for either a 24-hour extension (and may stack tokens to obtain 48-hour extensions, etc.) or revise a journal which was assessed at an R or X in order to obtain an M.
  • The Rubik's cube MFA can be attempted up to twice/week starting on January 27, without penalty, until you achieve the grade you are aiming for.
  • Details on other MFAs are forthcoming, but you may use tokens to revise and resubmit each MFA.

Question 2.0.11. Wait, what are tokens? What are they good for?
Tokens are a fake currency which you can use to break the rules of the class. You'll start the semester with five (5) tokens. You may have the opportunity to earn more, but this is not guaranteed. Tokens left over at the end of the semester are absorbed into the spacetime continuum and do you no good. In general, you may use tokens to:
  • earn a 24-hour extension on assignments with due dates. Tokens may be stacked to earn 48-hour extensions, 72-hour extensions, etc.;
  • revise a journal or MFA (other than the Rubik's cube MFA, which comes with two free attempts/week from January 27 until the last day of classes);
  • do other things, as announced (and updated here).
