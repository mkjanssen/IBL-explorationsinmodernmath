Discussion 7.1.4.12.

Godel's theorems leave mathematicians and students of mathematics in a precarious state. Consider the following questions for discussion.

  1. Godel's first theorem implies that that are mathematical questions that do not have “one right answer”. What do you think of this when you consider the nature of mathematics and the ideas you've grown up with regarding what sort of discipline mathematics is?
  2. Godel's second theorem implies that the consistency of mathematics is taken as an article of faith. Yet our society seems to have an elevated role for quantitative arguments (e.g., how does one refute a statistic?). How can these theorems help us to think about the proper place of mathematics in society?
