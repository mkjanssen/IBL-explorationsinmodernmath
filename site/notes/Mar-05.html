Section 9.1 Mar. 5: Swing Votes and Edge Cases

Subsection 9.1.1 Swing Votes

Since most states abide by the winner-take-all rule, single states can swing the outcome of an election from one candidate to another. This was especially true in the 2000 presidential election as we have seen previously. The 2016 election was not as simple, as the only state that could have singlehandedly changed the outcome was Texas, and it was not terribly close.

Exploration 9.1.1.

For the questions that follow consider the votes in this spreadsheet.

  1. In the 2016 presidential election in the state of Texas, what was the total difference in the numbers of popular votes cast for Clinton and Trump? Counting only votes cast for Clinton and Trump in Texas, what was the percentage difference in the numbers of popular votes cast for these two candidates?
  2. Which state was the closest? Does it depend on whether you are considering the vote difference as an actual number of votes or an overall percentage?
  3. In the 2016 election, what is the smallest number of voters in Texas who could have changed the outcome of the election by changing their votes from Trump to Clinton?
  4. (Harder) In the 2016 election, what is the smallest number of voters who could have changed their votes nationwide to swing the election from Clinton to Trump?

Subsection 9.1.2 Edge Cases

In software engineering, an edge case is a use/outcome that is not particularly likely to happen, but nonetheless could happen and thus still needs to be planned for. In the explorations to follow, we'll consider some extreme edge cases.

Exploration 9.1.3.
  1. Assuming that the voter turnout in each state and DC is exactly 60.2% of citizens of voting age, and that each voter cast a vote for either Clinton or Trump, construct a scenario in which Trump could have won the election while receiving less than 25% of the nationwide popular vote.
  2. In your answer to the previous part, which states did you assume Trump would win? Why did you choose those states?
  3. If you wanted to construct a winning coalition of states for Trump that contained the smallest number of popular votes, should you include more larger states or more smaller states? Give a convincing argument to justify your answer.
  4. What assumptions did you make in order to construct your scenario for the first question? Were they reasonable? Why or why not?

Subsection 9.1.3 If not the electoral college, then what?

Discussion 9.1.2.
Now that we've explored it a bit, how do you feel about the electoral college?

There are several proposed alternatives to the electoral college, including:

  1. Whoever wins the popular vote (with a plurality) wins.
  2. States could split their votes (like Maine and Nebraska already do).
  3. Proportional system: states could split their electoral votes based on the popular vote proportion earned in the state, so e.g., a candidate with 35.7% of the vote gets exactly 35.7% of the electors.

What do you think of the alternatives proposed above? What are their positive and negative features? Should one replace the electoral college? Or should we continue with things as they are?
