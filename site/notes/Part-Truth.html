Part II Truth

Throughout scripture, Christians are exhorted in various ways and to various activities regarding truth. We are told that knowledge of "the truth" will "set [us] free" 1 , Christ calls Himself "the way, the truth, and the life" 2 , and says that his mission is to testify to the truth 3 . Further, Paul exhorts believers to think on "whatever is true" 4 . But what is truth? Can mathematics help us better apprehend and understand it?

John 8:32
John 14:6
John 18:37; to which Pilate asks: "What is truth?"
Phil. 4:8

Mathematics is often thought of as one of the last bastions of objective truth. One widely accepted cultural norm is that if a statement is quantitative, or can be arrived at via a sequence of logical deductions, it is often considered "true", whereas statements that cannot be presented in such a way live in the realm of opinion. But what do mathematicians mean when they talk of a statement being "true"? The answer might surprise you.

In this unit, we'll explore inductive and deductive reasoning, as well as formal logic. We'll explore the shortcomings of mathematical thinking in addition to its strengths.

This chapter is heavily influenced by Discovering the Art of Mathematics: Truth, Reasoning, Certainty, and Proof by Fleron, Hotchkiss, Ecke, and von Renesse.

Learning Goals.

In this thematic unit, we will explore the following big ideas.

  • Inductive vs. deductive reasoning, truth vs. fact, and the strengths and weaknesses of each
  • Axiomatic systems, logical connectives, and formal logic
  • The search for complete and consistent logical foundations for mathematics in the late 19th/early 20th centuries
  • Set theory and infinite cardinalities, including Russell's paradox and the continuum hypothesis
  • The consequences of Godel's theorems
