Activity 4.3.2.

Try to write down/clarify the general reasoning patterns you used to prove the preceding theorems.

Axiom 4.3.4.
Consider the axioms of Smullyan Island 1 :
  1. All citizens of Smullyan Island are either knights or knaves.
  2. Knights always tell the truth.
  3. Knaves always lie.
Exploration 4.3.3.

You find yourself on Smullyan Island, talking to two citizens, Priyanka and Gretchen. Gretchen says, "We are both knaves."

  1. Can Gretchen be a knight? Explain.
  2. What can you conclude about the validity of Gretchen's statement? Explain.
  3. Deductively establish whether Priyanka is a knight or a knave.
Solution

If Gretchen were a knight, she would be lying when she said "We are both knaves." Thus, she is a knave. Since she is a knave, her statement must be false, which means that they are not both knaves. Therefore, Priyanka is a knight.

A hallmark of mathematical thinking and research is to abstract from a collection of examples to make a deeper structural conjecture, for which we then attempt to supply a deductive proof. As an example, consider Exploration 4.1.3. We collected examples of a pattern (the equations) and suggested the conjecture that all even whole numbers greater than or equal to 6 can be written as the sum of two primes.

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, mathematicians began mathematize and formalize logic itself. Today we begin to explore these foundational issues. We'll start with some definitions.

Definition 4.3.5.

A proposition is a declarative sentence which is either true or false, but not both. An elementary proposition is a sentence with a subject and a verb, but no connectives (such as and, or, not, if-then, or if-and-only-if).

Activity 4.3.4.

Determine which of the following are propositions (elementary or otherwise). If a given sentence is a statement, determine its truth value. If it isn't, explain why not.

  1. Erik Hoekstra is the president of Dordt University.
  2. The square root of a whole number is always a whole number.
  3. The Green Bay Packers are the worst football team.
  4. Why is this class so much fun?
  5. This sentence is false.
  6. A group of crows is called a murder.
  7. Everyone likes cats.
Solution
  1. This is a true proposition.
  2. This is a false proposition. As an example, \(\sqrt{21}\) is not a whole number.
  3. This one is harder (though it's an easy "true" from your instructor). But it's likely either a false proposition (due to the Packers not having the worst record in the league) or not a proposition at all, as agreement with the sentence is a matter of subjective taste.
  4. This is an interrogative, not a declaration.
  5. This is a declarative sentence, but it's neither true nor false; it's a paradox.
  6. This is a true proposition! Who knew?
  7. This is a false proposition.

Now that we have a sense for what a proposition is, we'll take old propositions and make new ones using logical connectives. In order to describe how the connectives work, mathematicians define the truth values of the new propositions formally in terms of the possible combinations of truth values from the constituent propositions. This gives us an abstract way of considering the truth values of propositions.

Definition 4.3.6.

Suppose \(P\) and \(Q\) are statements (e.g., like those in Activity 4.3.4). The negation of \(P\text{,}\) denoted \(\neg P\) and read "not \(P\)", has the opposite truth value of \(P\) and is defined by Table 4.3.7.

The conjunction of \(P\) and \(Q\text{,}\) denoted \(P \land Q\) and read "\(P\) and \(Q\)", is true when both \(P\) and \(Q\) are true, and false otherwise. See Table 4.3.8.

The disjunction of \(P\) and \(Q\text{,}\) denoted \(P \lor Q\) and read "\(P\) or \(Q\)", is true when \(P\) is true, \(Q\) is true, or both are true, and false otherwise. See Table 4.3.9.

Activity 4.3.5.

Meaningfully negate the following propositions (without just saying "It is not the case that...").

  1. \(e\) is a negative real number.
  2. Iowa is the tenth largest state.
  3. 17 is a prime number.
Solution
  1. \(e\) is a nonnegative real number.
  2. Iowa is not the tenth largest state.
  3. 17 is a composite number.
Exploration 4.3.6.

Determine the truth values of the following propositions.

  1. Math 149 meets on Mondays and the capital of Iowa is Des Moines.
  2. Math 149 meets on Mondays and the capital of Minnesota is Minneapolis.
  3. Math 149 meets on Mondays or the capital of Minnesota is Minneapolis.
Solution

We want to emphasize that the truth of the conjunction or disjunction depends only on the truth of the constituent statements. It doesn't matter whether it makes sense semantically to combine the constituents this way.

  1. This is true, as both constituents are true.
  2. This is false; the capital of Minnesota is St. Paul, not Minneapolis.
  3. This is true, since it's a disjunctive statement and one of the constituents is true (Math 149 meets on Mondays).

The last connective we'll consider (for now) is implication.

Definition 4.3.10.

Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be statements. The implication, "\(P\) implies \(Q\)" (or "if \(P\text{,}\) then \(Q\)") is denoted \(P\Rightarrow Q\text{,}\) and is false only when \(P\) is true but \(Q\) is false. See Table 4.3.11.

Exploration 4.3.7.

Determine the truth values of the following statements. Identify which row of Table 4.3.11 you are in.

  1. If \(x\) is negative, then \(-5x\) is positive.
  2. If we have Math 149 today, then it is Wednesday.
  3. If \(9 > 5\text{,}\) then dogs do not have wings.
  4. If \(2=4\text{,}\) then Calvin Coolidge is our greatest president.

The formalization of mathematical logic ramps up a bit when we consider conditional statements. It is important to remember that we define the truth value of the proposition \(P \Rightarrow Q\) purely formally based on the structure of the conditional statement and the truth values of the constituents \(P\) and \(Q\text{.}\) There need not be a causal relationship between \(P\) and \(Q\text{!}\)

The last two rows of Table 4.3.11 are also worth a moment of our time. They state that if the statement \(P\) 2  is false, then the implication \(P\Rightarrow Q\) is true. Note that this is different than saying that \(Q\) 3  is true. When the implication \(P\Rightarrow Q\) is true because \(P\) is false, we usually say that \(P\Rightarrow Q\) is vacuously true.
