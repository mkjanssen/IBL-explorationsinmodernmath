Section 3.5 January 24, 2020: Magic Cube Moves II

Last time, we saw two "magic" Cube moves that effect only certain cubies. We didn't get a chance to talk about the second one, so it was moved to today. We'll then explore two more given time.

Exploration 3.5.1.

Last time, we explored Cube Move 3.4.2, but didn't quite finish Exploration 3.4.3. We'll finish that as a class first before moving on to Exploration 3.5.2.

Solution

We clarified that this move cycles three corners on the front face: \(ufl \to ufr \to dfr \to ufl\text{.}\) All other cubies are left alone, and the orientations of the cycled cubies remain unchanged. After some discussion, the name "Three Corner Tango" seemed to stick.

Cube Move 3.5.1.
Consider the following sequence of cube moves:
\begin{equation*} \text{Corner Bulldog} = (R^{-1} D^2 R B^{-1} U^2 B)^2. \end{equation*}
Written less compactly, this move is: \(R^{-1} D^2 R B^{-1} U^2 B R^{-1} D^2 R B^{-1} U^2 B\text{.}\)
Exploration 3.5.2.

Consider Cube Move 3.5.1.

  1. Which cubies are affected by this move?
  2. By performing this move several times, identify on the diagram below the effects the move has on the Cube.
  3. Using the cubie notation described in Convention 3.4.1, describe what is happening to the Cube.
  4. Given your answer to the previous question, exercise your agency as image-bearers and suggest a two- or three-letter name/abbreviation for this move.
  5. What is the order of the move?
Solution

The cubies affected by this move are \(ufr\) and \(dbl\text{.}\) This move reorients the affected cubies, and since the cubies are corners, it has order 3. Due to the effects on the \(ufr\) cubie, which has \(fur\) as an anagram, the name Corner Bulldog was suggested.

Convention 3.5.2.

By \(M_R\) we mean the clockwise rotation of the middle layer as you look at the right face of the Cube. That is, \(M_R\) rotates the middle layer parallel to \(R\text{.}\)

Cube Move 3.5.3.
Consider the following sequence of Cube moves:
\begin{equation*} \text{Three Edge Tango} = M_R^2 U^{-1} M_R^{-1} U^2 M_R U^{-1} M_R^2 \end{equation*}
Exploration 3.5.3.

Consider Cube Move 3.5.3.

  1. By performing this move several times, identify on the diagram below what is happening to the Cube.
  2. Using the cubie notation described in Convention 3.4.1, describe what is happening to the Cube.
  3. Given what happens to the Cube, exercise your agency as image-bearers and suggest a short name/abbreviation for this move.
  4. What is the order of the move?
Solution

This moves cycles three edge cubies clockwise on the up face:

\begin{equation*} uf \to ul \to ur \to uf. \end{equation*}

Since it's a cycle of three cubies, it has order 3. Following our name for Cube Move 3.4.2, we suggested the name Three Edge Tango. We also observed that if you reverse the direction of the upper rotations, you move the same three cubies, but in counterclockwise fashion. That is, the move

\begin{equation*} M_R^2 U M_R^{-1} U^2 M_R U M_R^2 \end{equation*}

moves

\begin{equation*} uf \to ur \to ul \to uf. \end{equation*}

We've investigated the three main cube moves: the Three Corner Tango (which cycles the position of three corners on the front face), the Three Edge Tango (which cycles the position of three edges on the up face), and the Corner Bulldog (which reorients two corners at opposite ends of the Cube). We're almost there! In fact, you can peek ahead at Cube Move 3.6.1 and try to solve your Cubes this weekend. But we'll spend the next two class periods working on mastering these moves and solving our Cubes using these moves.
