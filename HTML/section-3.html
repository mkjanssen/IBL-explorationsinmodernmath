Section 3.3 January 20, 2020: Algebraic Notation and Order

Today, we'll get up to speed on how far everyone has come in solving their white (top) faces and layers. We'll be particularly interested in how you talk about/describe your methods. Then we'll see how the Cubing community has taken to writing and notating methods for systematically describing the motion of the Cube.

Discussion 3.3.1.

Find a partner. Take turns describing verbally and without pointing to your Cube your method for moving a corner cubie into its proper position. Similarly, verbally describe your method for moving an edge cubie into its proper position.

Solution

We identified a few strategies for moving corner cubies to the correct location. If the cubie in question was already in the correct layer and the only thing we cared about was putting it in the correct location, we could just turn the appropriate layer until it was in the correct position.

If it wasn't in the correct layer, we can orient the Cube and perform a rotation or two so the correct position for the cubie is the top right and the cubie in question is in the bottom left position. We then turn the right layer toward us, rotate the bottom layer so that the correct cubie is now in the front bottom right, and then turn the right layer away from us so that it is now in the correct location.

We can also guess and check. \(\ddot\smile\)

Discussion 3.3.2.
As you probably noticed, it is challenging to orally describe complex/technical ideas in much detail
A former professor of mine often threw up her hands at the idea of doing "math in the air".
. Is it easier to describe your cubie movements in writing? Why or why not?
Definition 3.3.3.

When the Cube is oriented so the white side is up and the red side is facing you, as in Figure 3.3.4, we will say it is in standard orientation.

Convention 3.3.5.

The standard algebraic notation for moving the faces of the Cube is as follows: \(F, R, L, U, D\text{,}\) and \(B\) refer to clockwise quarter-turns (\(90^\circ\) rotations) of the front, right, left, up, down, and back faces, respectively, as you look at the face in question.

On the other hand, a \(-1\) superscript indicates a counterclockwise turn as you look at the face in question.

Finally, we can string moves together by writing them left to right, such as \(R F U L D\text{.}\) If we want to repeat a move, we use exponents in the usual way, such as \(R F^2 U (LD)^3\text{.}\)

Exploration 3.3.1.

Rather than refer to the faces of the Cube by their orientation, we could have referred to them by color. Compare and contrast the color naming scheme to the orientation naming scheme. What advantages/disadvantages do each present?

Solution

We had a few thoughts. First, not every Cube has the standard coloring that ours do. Second, some people are colorblind, so referring to the faces by color would not be helpful. In short, every Cube has six sides and an orientation when you hold it in front of you that makes it easy to identify the up, down, back, front, left, and right faces. So that is what the Cubing community has adopted.

A final concept we'll explore today is that of order.

Definition 3.3.6.

The order of a sequence of moves \(M\) is the least positive number of times \(n\) that \(M\) can be repeated before your Cube is back where it started. We write \(| M | = n\text{.}\)

Example 3.3.7.

The order of \(R\) is 4, since 4 quarter-turns will put my right face back where it started, and no smaller postive number of turns will.

Exploration 3.3.2.

Some questions on algebraic notation and order.

  1. How do \(R\) and \(L\) differ? (Other than that they move different sides of the Cube, of course.)
  2. How are \(R^3\) and \(R^{-1}\) related?
  3. What is \(|F^2|\text{?}\)
  4. What is \(|R D R^{-1} D^{-1}|\text{?}\)
An algebraic expression of the form \(XYX^{-1}Y^{-1}\) is called a commutator. Commutators live deep in the realm of abstract algebra, and have applications as diverse as solving polynomial equations and understanding concepts in quantum mechanics.
Solution
  1. Since the \(R\) and \(L\) faces mirror one another, a clockwise right turn pushes the right layer of the front face to the top, while a clockwise turn of the left face pushes the left layer of the front face to the bottom.
  2. They're the same in the sense that applying either one has the same effect on the cube.
  3. It's 2. We know that \(F^2\) turns the front face twice in the clockwise direction; if we repeat that move, we'll be back where we started.
  4. We saw that \(|R D R^{-1} D^{-1}| = 6\) by repeating the sequence of four moves over and over again until we got back to where we started. We had to do it 6 times before that was the case.
Conclusion.

After a week away, we started exploring algebraic notation for describing our Cube moves. Notation is an incredibly powerful mathematical tool. In a few symbols, we can communicate a lot of information. This can be counterproductive if we are not careful to define what our notation means. We'll become more familiar with cubing notation as we explore some "magical" Cube moves next time.
