Section 3.4 January 22, 2020: Magic Cube Moves I

In order to solve the Cube, we need to figure out how to move cubies in carefully controlled ways. Starting in this section, we'll introduce one move at a time and make sure we understand exactly what it does before moving on.

As a warning, be aware that what follows might be occasionally frustrating. That's ok! As we saw last time, the order in which moves are executed does matter, so one mistake will throw everything off. You may want to designate one member of the group as the Cube turner, and let the others carefully track the moving cubies.

Convention 3.4.1.

We identify a cubie by the faces on which it sits, using lowercase letters, and regardless of how our Cube is oriented. So, for instance, the corner cubie on the upper, front, right face is identified by the notation ufr. The order in which the faces are specified does not matter.

Activity 3.4.1.

Consider the so-called superflip configuration below.

In the superflip, every cubie is in the correct location, but the edges are flipped.

  1. Name the position of the edge cubie with an orange and blue facelet.
  2. What name would we give to the green center cubie in this configuration?
  3. Identify the colors of the cubie in the \(\ell db\) position.
Solution

We first observed that we need three letters to identify corner cubies, two letters to identify edge cubies, and one to identify center cubies. These numbers correspond to the number of facelets each type of cubie has. In practice, we won't have much reason to refer to center cubies by name, as the centers don't move relative to one another.

  1. This cubie is in the \(fl\) position.
  2. We could call this cubie \(r\text{.}\)
  3. The colors are red, blue, and yellow.
Investigation 3.4.2. Cube Moves and the Commutative Property.

Suppose \(X\) and \(Y\) are arbitrary Cube moves. Is it the case that \(X\) and \(Y\) must always commute? That is, is it true that \(XY = YX\) for all choices of Cube moves? If yes, explain why. If it's not always true, give an example. In that case, are there conditions you can put on \(X\) and \(Y\) for which it is true?

Solution

In general, if the faces we're rotating, say \(R\) and \(D\text{,}\) have any cubies in common, the order in which we rotate matters. In this example, \(RD \ne DR\text{.}\) However, if the faces have no cubies in common, i.e., they are opposite one another, the order they are rotated does not matter. So, e.g., \(RL = LR\text{.}\)

We now turn to a couple of "mathemagical" Cube moves.

Cube Move 3.4.2.
Consider the following sequence of Cube moves:
\begin{equation*} \text{Three Corner Tango} = U^{-1} R^{-1} D^{-1} R U R^{-1} D R \end{equation*}
Exploration 3.4.3.

The Cube move described in Cube Move 3.4.2 does something to the front face of the Cube.

  1. By performing this move several times, identify on the diagram below what is happening to the front face of the Cube.
  2. Using the cubie notation described in Convention 3.4.1, describe what is happening to the front face of the Cube.
  3. Given what happens to the front face, exercise your agency as image-bearers and suggest a short name/abbreviation for this move.
  4. What is the order of the move?
Solution

We had barely enough time to start talking about this exploration when class ended. It was suggested that the effect of this move on the front face of the Cube is

\begin{equation*} ufl \to dfr \to ufr \to ufl. \end{equation*}

Do we agree?

Activity 3.4.4.

Now that you have identified exactly what Cube Move 3.4.2 does, use it to solve as much of your Cube as it allows.

Conclusion.

We started by settling on notation to use to identify cubie locations. Our convention is to use lowercase letters referring to the faces in which the cubie sits, so corners require three letters, edges two, and centers one.

We then looked at Cube Move 3.4.2. After some trial and error, we think we determined that it moves three corners on the front face in a cyclical manner. Which three corners it moves, and in what order, was not entirely settled when class ended. We'll pick this question up next time.
