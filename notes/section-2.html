Section 3.2 January 13, 2020: All About Cubies

The Rubik's cube 1  was invented in 1974 by Erno Rubik, a Hungarian sculptor and professor of architecture. It won the Spiel de Jahres (the German game of the year) in 1980, and it became one of the best-selling toys of all time. It's a great place to begin our exploration of mathematical play.

We'll call it the Cube for short.
Investigation 3.2.1.

In your groups, investigate your Cubes. What do you notice? What do you wonder? Make a list of as many observations and questions as you can, and write them on your shared whiteboard.

Solution

We noticed several things: there are six sides, and six colors. It seems that the white center is opposite the yellow, the blue center is opposite the green, and the red center is opposite the orange.

The main thing we wondered is: why does this exist? Why did Erno Rubik invent it? One person suggested that, well, it's a puzzle. And like we talked about last time, humans have this innate desire for play and creativity that was placed there by God. While some people might find the Cube a fun puzzle to play with, others might prefer a different sort of puzzle or game.

Activity 3.2.2.

Just by turning the sides of the Cube, there are over 43 quintillion different configurations possible.

43,252,003,274,489,856,000 to be exact.
This is a nigh unimaginably large number. Brainstorm as many ways as you can (at least two!) to make this number concrete.

Solution

A popular approach involved covering the earth with a carpet of 43 quintillion objects. The estimates were that 43 quintillion pennies would carpet the earth 86 times, while 43 quintillion Rubik's cubes would blanket the earth 275 times. Another group estimated that 43 quintillion seconds is over a trillion years.

Either way, it's huge!

Definition 3.2.1.

The little cubes which make up the Cube are often called cubies. The cubies located at the corners are imaginatively called corner cubies, the cubies located at the centers center cubies, and the others edge cubies.

Investigation 3.2.3.

In this investigation we'll consider the corner cubies. Hold your Cube with the white center cubie facing up and the red center cubie facing you.

  1. How many corners are there?
  2. How many stickers does a corner cubie have?
  3. Can you move a corner cubie to a position other than a corner? Explain your reasoning.
  4. Identify all the positions on the Cube to which a corner cubie can be moved while keeping the white center cubie on top and red in front.

As always, make sure you can explain your answers.

Solution
There are 8 corners, with three stickers each. Since all corners have three stickers, and other cubies have fewer stickers, we can't move a corner cubie to any location other than another corner. But we can move a given corner to any other corner.
Investigation 3.2.4.

In this investigation we'll consider the edge cubies. Hold your Cube with the white center cubie facing up and the red center cubie facing you.

  1. How many edge cubies are there?
  2. How many stickers does an edge cubie have?
  3. Can you move an edge cubie to a position other than an edge? Explain your reasoning.
  4. Identify all the positions on the Cube to which an edge cubie can be moved while keeping the white center cubie on top and red in front.

As always, make sure you can explain your answers.

Solution
There are twelve edge cubies, with two stickers each. As was the case with corners, we can only put edge cubies in other edge locations, but we can move a given edge cubie to any other edge.
Investigation 3.2.5.

In this investigation we'll consider the center cubies. Hold your Cube with the white center cubie facing up and the red center cubie facing you.

  1. How many center cubies are there?
  2. How many stickers does an center cubie have?
  3. Can you move a center cubie to a position other than a center? Explain your reasoning.
  4. Identify all the positions on the Cube to which a center cubie can be moved while keeping the white center cubie on top and red in front.

As always, make sure you can explain your answers.

Solution
Finally, we saw that the six corners (each with one sticker) cannot move relative to the others.
Question.

How can you tell when a cubie is in the correct location? How can you tell when it is oriented correctly? Is there a difference? 2 

Does it depend on the cubie?
Definition 3.2.2.

A face of the Cube refers to one of its sides. We say a face is solved if all the stickers on that side are the same color.

Challenge.

Scramble your Cube. Then solve the white face (this may take hours or days, not minutes!). When you solve, it congratulations! Now scramble it and do it again.

Once you can reliably solve the white face, describe, in writing, your methods as clearly and precisely as you can.

Challenge.

Define the white layer.

Scramble your cube, and solve the white layer. Again, this may take hours or days. When you solve it, congratulations! Scramble it and do it again.

Once you can reliably solve the white layer, describe, in writing, your methods as clearly and precisely as you can.

Conclusion.

Today we investigated our Cubes and explored the various properties of the cubies of which the Cubes are composed. We saw that there are three types: centers, edges, and corners. Since the centers don't move relative to one another, we can use them to determine when a given cubie is in the correct location.

Ideally, your strategy will be clear enough that you could hand it to a friend who knows nothing about the Cube and they could follow your instructions to solve it. If you accomplish this, try to do the same for the white layer. If you don't accomplish it, that's okay! We'll get there!
