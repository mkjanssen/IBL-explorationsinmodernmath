Section 5.2 Feb. 10: Statements and Logical Connectives

Today we carefully describe the fundamental aspects of matheamtical reasoning, beginning with a visit to Smullyan Island 1 . We'll then consider propositions and their truth values, as well as ways of combining elementary propositions into more complex propositions via logical connectives. One of the most important and subtle connectives is the conditional implication, described in Definition 5.2.7.

Named for American mathematician and logician, Raymond Smullyan.
Axiom 5.2.1.
Consider the axioms of Smullyan Island:
  1. All citizens of Smullyan Island are either knights or knaves.
  2. Knights always tell the truth.
  3. Knaves always lie.
Exploration 5.2.1.

You find yourself on Smullyan Island, talking to two citizens, Priyanka and Gretchen. Gretchen says, "We are both knaves."

  1. Can Gretchen be a knight? Explain.
  2. What can you conclude about the validity of Gretchen's statement? Explain.
  3. Deductively establish whether Priyanka is a knight or a knave.

A hallmark of mathematical thinking and research is to abstract from a collection of examples to make a deeper structural conjecture, for which we then attempt to supply a deductive proof. As an example, consider Exploration 4.2.2. We collected examples of a pattern (the equations) and suggested the conjecture that all even whole numbers greater than or equal to 6 can be written as the sum of two primes.

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, mathematicians began mathematize and formalize logic itself. Today we begin to explore these foundational issues. We'll start with some definitions.

Definition 5.2.2.

A proposition is a declarative sentence which is either true or false, but not both. An elementary proposition is a sentence with a subject and a verb, but no connectives (such as and, or, not, if-then, or if-and-only-if).

Activity 5.2.2.

Determine which of the following are propositions (elementary or otherwise). If a given sentence is a statement, determine its truth value. If it isn't, explain why not.

  1. Erik Hoekstra is the president of Dordt University.
  2. The square root of a whole number is always a whole number.
  3. The Green Bay Packers are the worst football team.
  4. Why is this class so much fun?
  5. This sentence is false.
  6. A group of crows is called a murder.
  7. Everyone likes cats.

Now that we have a sense for what a proposition is, we'll take old propositions and make new ones using logical connectives. In order to describe how the connectives work, mathematicians define the truth values of the new propositions formally in terms of the possible combinations of truth values from the constituent propositions. This gives us an abstract way of considering the truth values of propositions.

Definition 5.2.3.

Suppose \(P\) and \(Q\) are statements (e.g., like those in Activity 5.2.2). The negation of \(P\text{,}\) denoted \(\neg P\) and read "not \(P\)", has the opposite truth value of \(P\) and is defined by Table 5.2.4.

The conjunction of \(P\) and \(Q\text{,}\) denoted \(P \land Q\) and read "\(P\) and \(Q\)", is true when both \(P\) and \(Q\) are true, and false otherwise. See Table 5.2.5.

The disjunction of \(P\) and \(Q\text{,}\) denoted \(P \lor Q\) and read "\(P\) or \(Q\)", is true when \(P\) is true, \(Q\) is true, or both are true, and false otherwise. See Table 5.2.6.

Activity 5.2.3.

Meaningfully negate the following propositions (without just saying "It is not the case that...").

  1. \(e\) is a negative real number.
  2. Iowa is the tenth largest state.
  3. 17 is a prime number.
Solution
  1. \(e\) is a nonnegative real number.
  2. Iowa is not the tenth largest state.
  3. 17 is a composite number.
Exploration 5.2.4.

Determine the truth values of the following propositions.

  1. Math 149 meets on Mondays and the capital of Iowa is Des Moines.
  2. Math 149 meets on Mondays and the capital of Minnesota is Minneapolis.
  3. Math 149 meets on Mondays or the capital of Minnesota is Minneapolis.

The last connective we'll consider (for now) is implication.

Definition 5.2.7.

Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be statements. The implication, "\(P\) implies \(Q\)" (or "if \(P\text{,}\) then \(Q\)") is denoted \(P\Rightarrow Q\text{,}\) and is false only when \(P\) is true but \(Q\) is false. See Table 5.2.8.

Exploration 5.2.5.

Determine the truth values of the following statements. Identify which row of Table 5.2.8 you are in.

  1. If \(x\) is negative, then \(-5x\) is positive.
  2. If we have Math 149 today, then it is Wednesday.
  3. If \(9 > 5\text{,}\) then dogs do not have wings.
  4. If \(2=4\text{,}\) then Calvin Coolidge is our greatest president.

The formalization of mathematical logic ramps up a bit when we consider conditional statements. It is important to remember that we define the truth value of the proposition \(P \Rightarrow Q\) purely formally based on the structure of the conditional statement and the truth values of the constituents \(P\) and \(Q\text{.}\) There need not be a causal relationship between \(P\) and \(Q\text{!}\)

The last two rows of Table 5.2.8 are also worth a moment of our time. They state that if the statement \(P\) 2  is false, then the implication \(P\Rightarrow Q\) is true. Note that this is different than saying that \(Q\) 3  is true. When the implication \(P\Rightarrow Q\) is true because \(P\) is false, we usually say that \(P\Rightarrow Q\) is vacuously true.

Often called the antecedent.
Often called the consequent.
Activity 5.2.6.

Suppose Dr. Janssen promises that, if everyone has solved their Rubik's Cube by Friday, then he will bring Defenders (on pretzel with everything) to class 4 . Unfortunately, a few students do not solve the Cube by Friday, so Dr. Janssen does not bring Defenders.

This is purely hypothetical.

Determine the truth value of the implication "If everyone can solve their Cubes by Friday, Dr. Janssen will bring Defenders to class". How does this help you think about vacuous truth?
