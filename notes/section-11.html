Section 5.1 February 21, 2020: Elections with Two Candidates

As you have hopefully seen thus far, this class is all about mathematical thinking, and its applications to realms of thought which we may not traditionally think of as mathematical. Specifically, we've applied mathematical thinking to play in the context of the Cube, truth in the context of deductive reasoning, and now justice in the context of voting and elections (and subsequently, human trafficking). Your final project will explore beauty in mathematics. More later.

Our main task in this chapter is to explore the mathematics of voting systems, which refers to both the ways votes are cast in elections and the way those votes are used to determine a winner. We'll see in this section that while two candidate-systems are straightforward relative to other voting systems, they provide a fertile ground to introduce and study characteristics we desire in all systems.

We first want to determine how each system treats the voters and the candidates. A fair system should, in theory, treat each voter and each candidate equally.

Activity 5.1.1.

It's time for the citizens of Sudden Valley to elect a new mayor, and they have two choices: Michael Bluth, and his brother, Buster Bluth. How should the winner be determined?

Investigation 5.1.2.

If Activity 5.1.1 seemed too easy, consider the following (likely alternative) suggestion.

The Bluths' father, George, has been a long-time player in Sudden Valley politics. It's important that all the citizens vote, but the winner will be whomever George votes for, regardless of how the other votes come in.

Of the 1001 citizens of Sudden Valley, suppose 1000 vote for Michael, and George votes for Buster. Who wins the election?

Solution

Buster wins!

The method described in Investigation 5.1.2 is known as a dictatorship, with George as the dictator.

Exploration 5.1.3.

Does a dictatorship treat all voters equally? Does it treat the candidates equally? Explain.

Solution

It does not. George's special status means the system treats him differently than the other voters.

Investigation 5.1.4.

If a dictatorship isn't your style, try this one: before the election runs and the citizens of Sudden Valley vote, it is decided that Buster will win. Does this method treat all of the voters equally? Does it treat the candidates equally? Explain.

Solution

Yes, all voters are treated equally. Each voter has the same amount of influence on the outcome. On the other hand, the candidates are not treated equally. One candidate is chosen ahead of time, and the other one has no chance, regardless how the votes come in.

The method described in Investigation 5.1.4 is known as imposed rule.

Let's try one more system on for size.

Investigation 5.1.5.

Consider the following system. Each citizen of Sudden Valley votes, and the votes are counted. The winner is the candidate with the smallest number of votes. This is known as minority rule.

  1. Suppose that the citizens of Sudden Valley vote, with 1000 voters voting for Michael and one (George, no longer a dictator) votes for Buster. Who wins under minority rule?
  2. Now suppose that George convinces 500 of the prospective Michael voters to switch and vote for Buster. Who wins in this case under minority rule?
  3. Does minority rule treat all voters equally? Does it treat all candidates equally? Explain.
  4. Under minority rule, is it beneficial or detrimental for a candidate to receive additional votes? Explain.
Solution
  1. Buster does. He got the fewest votes.
  2. Michael does, with 500 votes to Buster's 501.
  3. It treats all voters equally (no single voter gets to decide the outcome) and treats all candidates equally (no candidate is inherently preferred by the system).
  4. It is detrimental, as Question 2 shows. A winning candidate can become a losing candidate.

In our quest for a mathematically just voting system, we will find it helpful to define certain desirable qualities for a fair and just system to satisfy. When we say that a voting system satisfies a certain criterion, we mean that it always satisfies it; that is, it can never violate that criterion. We will refer to these important criteria as fairness criteria. We have already observed a few important criteria that we now formalize.

Definition 5.1.1.

A voting system for a two-candidate election is anonymous if it treats all voters equally. That is, if any two voters switched their votes, the outcome of the election should remain the same.

A voting system for a two-candidate election is neutral if it treats both candidates equally. That is, if every voter changes their vote, the outcome of the election should also change.

A voting system for a two-candidate election is monotone if is impossible for a winning candidate to become a losing candidate by gaining votes (and not losing any others), or for a losing candidate to become a winning candidate by losing votes (and not gaining any others).

Investigation 5.1.6.

Now suppose three members of the Bluth family are voting to determine who should take over the family business, The Bluth Company. The candidates are Michael and Buster. In order to vote, their friend, Barry, has devised a voting system. Three possible combinations of votes by Lucille, Lindsay, and Tobias are presented in Table 5.1.2.

  1. Which of the three properties described in Definition 5.1.1 are satisfied by Barry's voting system? Explain.
  2. Is Barry's sytem equivalent to any of the other three systems we've investigated thus far? Why or why not?
Solution
  1. None of the three properties are satisfied. Rows 1 and 3 demonstrate that Barry's system isn't anonymous; rows 1 and 2 demonstrate that it's not monotone; and rows 2 and 3 demonstrate that it's not neutral.
  2. No. If it were imposed rule, the winner would always be the same. If anyone were a dictator, they would vote for the winner each time. And it's not minority rule, as Michael wins in row 3 despite Buster getting a minority of the vote.
Investigation 5.1.7.

Let's investigate the three criteria in Definition 5.1.1 in relation to the three voting systems we've examined.

  1. Which of the three fairness criteria are satisfied by dictatorships? Explain clearly for each of your answers.
  2. Which of the three fairness criteria are satisfied by imposed rule? Explain clearly for each of your answers.
  3. Which of the three fairness criteria are satisfied by minority rule? Explain clearly for each of your answers.
Solution
Here is what we came up with.

The two-candidate voting system we haven't yet explored is probably the one that you actually suggested in Activity 5.1.1. Perhaps it is something like:

Each voter should vote for the candidate they want to win the election. The votes are counted, and the candidate with the largest number of votes should be declared the winner.
We'll worry about ties separately.

This is known as majority rule.

Investigation 5.1.8.

Which of the three fairness criteria does majority rule satisfy? Give a clear and convincing explanation of your answers.

Solution

All of them! It behaves much the same way as minority rule, except now it's helpful to get more votes.

In fact, for elections with two candidates, majority rule is the only system to satisfy all three fairness criteria.

Theorem 5.1.4 (K. May (1952)).

In a two-candidate election with an odd number of voters, majority rule is the only voting system that is anonymous, neutral, and monotone and avoids the possibility of a tie.

Thus, for elections with two candidates, there is a clear just choice for a voting system subject to our adopted fairness criteria: majority rule. However, as we know, most elections don't have just two candidates, so we will next consider how to handle situations with more than two choices. We'll refine the existing fairness criteria to handle three or more candidates, and develop additional criteria due to surprising situations that can arise.
