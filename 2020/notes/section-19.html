Section 7.3 April 20-27: Graph Theory and Human Trafficking

In this section, we'll introduce no new mathematics. As you may have noticed, accurate data on trafficking is sparse and often unreliable, so we use mathematics to determine the most likely hubs of human trafficking, and highway maps to explore how trafficked individuals may be moved about the country.

The activities in this unit are a bit longer and more open-ended than some we've done. To share the workload, there are jobs for up to four people in each group, named as persons A-D. Note that person D's tasks are optional, as you do not all have four group members. Ensure that you identify your role early in the week so that you can be available to chime in when you need to.

Exploration 7.3.1.

According to most recent UNDOC reports, most human trafficking victims to the United States originated from Latin America, the Caribbean, and Asia. In order to see how they are likely arriving, we'll need some flight maps.

  1. Locate the flight (route) maps for airlines as follows. Person A: 1 United States airline, 1 Latin American (not Mexican or Caribbean) airline, 1 Caribbean airline; Person B: 1 United States airline (different from Person A's), 1 Mexican airline, and 1 Asian airline. Clearly state your airlines, which country they originate from, and provide a link (or upload) to their route maps.
  2. Persons A and B: You may have noticed that the flight maps have the appearance of graphs, with the edges representing a particular flight segment, and the vertices the cities between which the planes fly. For each airline you found, list the top 10 U.S. cities they fly to, ordered by (vertex) degree (see Definition 7.1.6). Make sure to state the degree of each city (and count flights between U.S. cities as well).
  3. Person D: record observations about the route maps and lists of cities and their degrees. Are there overlaps in the lists? Are the high-degree cities connected?
  4. Person C: If a city appears in multiple lists from Question 2, add its degrees from the various lists together (e.g., if Seattle has degree 7 in one list, degree 10 in another, and degree 15 in a third, its new total degree is \(7+10+15 = 32\)). Use these sums of degrees to determine the top 4 hubs for the transportation of people into the United States. What do you notice about the hubs? Are they in similar regions of the United States? Are any cities hubs for multiple countries (refer to the route maps from Question 1)?

Once trafficked people are in the country, they are generally moved via interstates and highways. In the next activity, we'll explore the major road system connecting one of your hubs to the surrounding region.

Exploration 7.3.2.

For this exploration, we'll use only the cities and roads identified on this map of the 48 contiguous United States. You can use Google Maps to calculate distances.

  1. Person C: Make sure you understand completely what you are being asked to do before you start! Choose one of your group's hubs identified in Exploration 7.3.1. Identify at least six other states nearby that hub's state; start with neighboring states, and expand to their neighbors until you have at least six states in addition to the state with the airline hub (feel free to do more). Then create a graph as follows.

    Each state is a single vertex. You should thus have at least 7 vertices.

    Let edges represent major highways (or combinations of major highways) that can be used to travel between states. There will likely be multiple edges connecting states! All travel will be between cities large enough to be listed on the map. Assign weights to be the approximate mileage to travel between the states via the specified routes as found on Google Maps. For example, the map shows that you can take Highway 5 to travel between Portland, OR, and Olympia, WA. This means there is an edge between OR and WA with weight 114 miles. However, you can also travel between Portland, OR, and Spokane, WA using Highways 84 and 90. This will add another edge to your graph with different mileage. (Since to move from, say, Washington to Montana requires travel through Idaho, and the distances are additive, we only need to consider routes between adjacent states.)

  2. Person B: Create a minimal spanning tree for your map using the elimination algorithm identified in Example 7.2.12. Assuming that traffickers only use major highways when transporting people, the minimal spanning minimizes the number of miles to each city. What are the major highways of importance? What states in your region are the most significant? Do the cities with the highest degree remain?
  3. Person A: Use Prim's algorithm (recall Algorithm 7.2.13) to create a minimal spanning tree. Compare this to your previous spanning tree.
  4. Person D: Summarize the results of the previous two questions. What are the major hubs in each state? Note that you need to think carefully about which route each edge represents.
