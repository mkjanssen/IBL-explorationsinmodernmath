Section 4.1 January 31, 2020: What is Truth? / Inductive Reasoning

Today we'll attempt to define truth, and then explore inductive reasoning.

Investigation 4.1.1.

What is truth? What is fact? How are the two concepts related? How are they different?

Activity 4.1.2. What's my world?

In the game What's my World?, one person thinks of a single law that defines a hypothetical world (e.g., ``My world does not contain things that start with the letter `C'.''). The other players attempt to guess this governing law by taking turns asking questions of the creator, such as ``Does your world contain dogs? Does it contain cats?'' and so on. When one of the guessers believe they can identify the governing law, they ask the creator.

Play a few rounds of this game, taking turns in the role of creator. Other than asking the creator directly, how could you be certain that you had determined the law of the world?

Definition 4.1.1.

Inductive reasoning is the process of drawing general conclusions from particular instances.

The work of the guesser in Activity 4.1.2 is to employ inductive reasoning to determine the general law put in place by the creator. This is the scientific process at its best: looking at the creation its orderliness and our creatureliness to infer larger governing principles. If you have taken a statistics course, this is the type of reasoning employed therein. If we have time in this unit, we may explore some examples of statistical reasoning.

But how does this work in mathematics?

Exploration 4.1.3.

Consider the following equations.

\begin{align*} 6 \amp = 3 + 3\\ 8 \amp = 3 + 5\\ 10 \amp = 5 + 5\\ 12 \amp = 5 + 7\\ \amp \vdots \end{align*}
  1. The vertical ellipsis \(\vdots\) appearing below the four equations suggests that the equations form a pattern that continues. What do you think the next five equations are in this pattern?
  2. Your answer to the previous question may fit the data, but it may not be what we are ultimately looking for. Let's impose another rule: the number 9 is never allowed. Can you find a (possibly different) pattern that fits this new clue in addition to the existing data?
  3. What if we further restrict ourselves by outlawing the use of 15 and any even numbers? Can you find a pattern that fits these new clues in addition to the equations above? What are the next five equations in this case?

Let's look at one more activity.

Activity 4.1.4.

In a recent paper, Lenny Jones introduced an interesting sequence of numbers:

\begin{align*} s_0 \amp = 12\\ s_1 \amp = 121\\ s_2 \amp = 1,211\\ s_3 \amp = 12,111\\ s_4 \amp = 121,111\\ \amp \vdots \end{align*}

Recall that a prime number is a number greater than 1 that is not divisible by any positive number other than 1 and itself. A number greater than 1 is composite if it's not prime.

  1. Are any of \(s_0, s_1, s_2, s_3\text{,}\) or \(s_4\) prime?
  2. Use computer software (like Wolfram|Alpha and the isPrime command) to determine which of the numbers in Table 4.1.2, if any, are prime.
  3. Do you think any of the numbers in the (infinite!) sequence \(s_0, s_1, s_2, \ldots\) are prime?
  4. Can you prove, beyond a shadow of a doubt, that all of the numbers \(s_1, s_3, s_5, \ldots\) are composite? How about the numbers \(s_0, s_3, s_6, \ldots\text{?}\)
  5. Each of the numbers \(s_0, s_1, \ldots, s_{100}\) are composite. Would you like to change your answer to Question 3?
  6. Write out \(s_{136}\text{.}\) Do you know anything about \(s_{136}\text{?}\)
  7. How long do you think it would take to check to see if \(s_{136}\) is prime?
Activity 4.1.5.

What are the strengths and weaknesses of inductive reasoning? Brainstorm as many as you can. What could you do to minimize the effect(s) of the weaknesses?

Mathematicians tend to be most interested in determining when a given property always holds for whatever collection of objects it purports to describe. Such properties are usually established deductively; we'll look at this next time.
