Section 4.4 February 10-19, 2020 : The Foundational Crisis of Mathematics

We have seen thus far a way of formalizing logic so that we can think about the truth of a statement purely syntactically (structurally) without regard for the semantic meaning of the statements under consideration. In the late 19th century, mathematicians developed what became known as formal systems, consisting of axioms, which were strings of symbols, such as

\begin{equation*} \exists B \forall C, (C\in B \Leftrightarrow C\subseteq A), \end{equation*}

along with a logical calculus, which govern the rules of inference that can be used on the axioms to deduce new theorems.

Further, mathematicians had long assumed there were consistent foundational axioms for their discipline. Newly discovered paradoxes challenged this view.

Exploration 4.4.1. Russell's Paradox.

In a certain town lives a barber who only cuts the hair of all people who do not cut their own hair. Who cuts the barber's hair?

Solution

This is a paradox: if the barber cuts his own hair, we reach a contradiction, as he only cuts the hair of those who do not cut their own hair. On the other hand, if the barber does not cut his own hair, he does by definition.

This sort of self-referential paradox is exactly the sort that we'll study later in this section that led to the foundational crisis referred to in the section title.

Two main schools of mathematical philosophy sprung up in the wake of these discoveries. The formalists argued that statements of mathematics and logic are really just about the rules and consequences for manipulating symbols and strings of letters. That is, mathematics does not have a subject matter at all--just empty symbols, which may be given an interpretation in particular situations and thus have meaning.

The response came from the intuitionists. Intuitionism is an approach that consideres mathematics to be purely the result of the constructive mental activity of humans rather than the discovery of any principles which we can reasonably claim exist in an objective reality. Thus, in some sense, mathematics is up to whoever is doing the mathematics. To the intuitionists, the formalists were reducing mathematics to a meaningless game with symbols.

Discussion 4.4.1.

Do you resonate more with a formalist approach to mathematics, or an intuitionist approach? Relatedly: do you believe mathematics is something humans invent, or something we discover in the creation? Why?

In 1900, at the second International Congress of Mathematicians in Paris, the esteemed mathematician David Hilbert posed 23 theretofore unanswered problems in mathematics that he thought were important to guide the development of mathematics in the 20th century. We've already seen one of them (the 8th): the Goldbach conjecture. Most of Hilbert's problems have been solved, but three are unresolved, two are thought to be too vague to ever get consensus on what a solution would look like, and one is the subject of much debate.

In an attempt to resolve the issues raised by paradoxes like Russell's Paradox, Hilbert posed this problem, the second on the list:

But above all I wish to designate the following as the most important among the numerous questions which can be asked with regard to the axioms: To prove that they are not contradictory, that is, that a definite number of logical steps based upon them can never lead to contradictory results….

That is, Hilbert wanted mathematicians to prove that the axioms on which mathematics was founded did not lead to a contradiction. The resolution to this problem is surprising, and to begin to explore it, we will return to Smullyan Island. Recall Axiom 4.3.4.

Exploration 4.4.2.

While visiting Smullyan Island, we meet two natives, Corrie and Will. Will says, "Corrie is a knave." Corrie says, "Exactly one of us is a knight." What are Corrie and Will?

Exploration 4.4.3.

While visiting Smullyan Island, you meet three people: Abbie, Bart, and Cate. Abbie says, "Cate is a knave". Bart says, "None of us are knaves." Cate says, "Bart is a knight." What are Abbie, Bart, and Cate?

For the explorations that follow, recall that the only way in which the implication \(P\Rightarrow Q\) is false is if \(P\) is true but \(Q\) is false; see Definition 4.3.10 and the ensuing discussion for more.

Exploration 4.4.5.

  2. Can a knight say, "If I am a knight, then \(1+1=3\text{?}\)" Explain.

Discussion 4.4.2.

If a native of Smullyan Island makes a statement of the form "If I am a knight, then \(Q\)" for some statement \(Q\text{,}\) what can you conclude about the native and the truth or falsity of \(Q\text{?}\) Why?

Solution

Since knaves can't say anything of this form, and knights can't say it when \(Q\) is false, we can conclude that the native is a knight and that \(Q\) is true.

Our exploration of the major developments in 20th century mathematical logic will hinge on a careful analysis of statements of the form "You will/will not believe that...". So, we turn to an initial analysis of such statements.

Our main focus of study in what follows is the statement "You will never believe that I am a knight," made by a native of Smullyan Island 2 . In order to draw appropriate conclusions, we will need to be outside the system, so we will consider a reasoner named Raymond as he interacts with natives on Smullyan Island. We will first deal with a Raymond who believes he is incapable of making a mistake; that is, if Raymond believes a particular statement, then, as far as he is concerned, the statement is really true. For example, if Raymond ever believes that a native is a knight, then because he thinks he is incapable of making mistakes, he concludes that the native really is a night and therefore that everything that person says is true.

Again, we owe a debt to the Discovering the Art of Mathematics project.

The primary takeaway from Exploration 4.4.8 is that it is possible, assuming the rules of Smullyan Island hold, for a person to both believe and not believe something. This is the heart of Godel's Incompleteness Theorems. Before we explore Godel's First Incompleteness Theorem, we make a definition.

Definition 4.4.3.

A reasoner is said to be inconsistent if there is some statement \(P\) such that the reasoner believes both \(P\) and \(\neg P\text{.}\) We call a reasoner consistent if there is no statement \(P\) such that the reasoner believes both \(P\) and \(\neg P\text{.}\)

Exploration 4.4.9. Godel's First Incompleteness Theorem via Knights and Knaves.

A normal 3  consistent stable 4  reasoner named Raymond visits Smullyan Island and meets a native who says, "You will never believe that I am a knight".

A reasoner is normal if whenever he believes a statement, then he believes that he believes it.
A reasoner is stable if whenever he believes that he believes a statement, then he believes it.
  1. Suppose that Raymond believes the native is a knight. Explain why it is reasonable to conclude that that Raymond believes that he believes the native is a knight.
  2. Since Raymond believes that the native is a knight, what must he also believe, based on what the native said? Explain.
  3. Explain why your answers to Questions 1-2 show that Raymond will thus never actually believe the native is a knight since he is consistent.
  4. Alternatively, suppose that Raymond at some point believes that the native is a knave. What must he also believe, based on what the native said? Explain
  5. Explain why your answers to questions 4-5 show that Raymond will thus never actually believe the native is a knave since he is consistent.
  6. Use your answers to questions 1-6 to explain why Raymond will never believe that the native is a knight nor that this native is a knave.
  7. Explain why, despite your answer to the previous question, we know that the native must be either a knight or a knave.

Exploration 4.4.9 illustrates Godel's First Incompleteness Theorem, which revolutionized 20th century mathematical logic.

Godel's First Incompleteness Theorem.

A normal, consistent, stable reasoner of type 1 5  comes to the island and believes the rules of the island. She meets a native who says: "You will never believe that I am a knight". Then the reasoner's belief system is incomplete. That is, the reasoner will never believe that the native is a knight, and will never believe that the native is not a knight.

A reasoner is type 1 if she believes all tautologies (propositions that can be established purely on the basis of truth tables for the logical connectives) and believes in modus ponens: whenever \(P\) and \(P\Rightarrow Q\) are true, \(Q\) is true.

Informally, this theorem states that any sufficiently strong consistent formal system contains undecidable statements. That is, there are sensible statements that one can make which cannot be proved from within the system. In that case, we may adopt the undecidable statement or its negation as an additional axiom without causing any contradiction.

It may not surprise you to learn that there is a second incompleteness theorem, which we now state.

Worksheet Worksheet

Godel's Second Incompleteness Theorem.

A reasoner of type 4 6  visits the island and meets a native who says: "You will never believe that I am a knight." Then, if the reasoner is consistent, she can never know that she is consistent. Stated another way, if the reasoner ever believes that she cannot be inconsistent, she will become inconsistent.

Definition omitted.

Informally, Godel's Second Incompleteness Theorem states that no consistent formal system can prove its own consistency. We thus obtain limitations on the formal axiomatic approach to mathematics. Any consistent set of axioms and rules of inference strong enough to include arithmetic must contain undecidable statements. Furthermore, no sufficiently strong formal system can establish its own consistency!

Discussion 4.4.4.

Godel's theorems leave mathematicians and students of mathematics in a precarious state. Consider the following questions in your groups.

  1. Godel's first theorem implies that that are mathematical questions that do not have "one right answer". What do you think of this? If you're a future teacher, what implications, if any, does this have for your work?
  2. Godel's second theorem implies that the consistency of mathematics is taken as an article of faith. This, despite the elevated role that mathematical proof and/or quantitative justifications have in modern society. Discuss.

The eagle-eyed reader might note that Godel's first incompleteness theorem implies that there are undecidable statements, but the statement of the theorem does not give an example of such a statement. In order to see such a statement in context, we'll turn to an exploration of infinity. First, a definition.

Definition 4.4.5.

Let \(S\) and \(T\) be sets, which we may think of as collections of objects. We say that \(S\) and \(T\) have the same cardinality if there is a one-to-one correspondence between the objects of \(S\) and those of \(T\text{,}\) i.e., if each element of \(S\) can be paired up with one and only one unique element of \(T\text{.}\) In this case, we write \(|S| = |T|\text{.}\)

  2. Can you find a one-to-one correspondence between \(S\) and \(U\text{?}\) Describe it, or explain why none exist.
  3. Can you find a one-to-one correspondence between \(T\) and \(U\text{?}\) Describe it, or explain why none exist.

In order to explore our undecidable statement, we need to set some notation.

Definition 4.4.6.

We define the following sets of numbers.

  1. The natural numbers are given by \(\mathbb{N} = \{1, 2, 3, \ldots\}\text{.}\)
  2. The whole numbers are given by \(\mathbb{W} = \{0,1,2,3,\ldots\}\text{.}\)
  3. The integers are given by \(\mathbb{Z} = \{\ldots, -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots\}\text{.}\)
  4. The even integers are given by \(2\mathbb{Z} = \{\ldots, -6, -4, -2, 0, 2, 4, 6, \ldots\}\text{.}\)
  5. The set of rational numbers is denoted by \(\mathbb{Q}\) and consists of all fractions \(\frac{a}{b}\text{,}\) where \(a,b\) are integers and \(b\ne 0\text{.}\)
  6. The set of real numbers is denoted by \(\mathbb{R}\) and is given by all positive and negative infinite decimals (alternatively, every point on the number line).
